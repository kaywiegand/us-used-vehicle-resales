Bad-Buy Prediction for Used-Vehicle Auctions

Predicting, before purchase, whether a car bought at auction will be a "Bad Buy" (a lemon that cannot be resold) — so a US used-car dealer can avoid the follow-up costs of a mistaken purchase.

The challenge

Only one in eight auctioned cars is a bad buy. On such an imbalanced target, accuracy is meaningless — a model that predicts "good buy" for everything is 88 % accurate and useless. The project therefore optimizes the F1 score of the bad-buy class and tunes the decision threshold explicitly.

Approach

Standard data-science workflow: exploration → preparation → modeling → evaluation. Price columns (the MMR family) are strongly correlated; features were engineered for price ratios, mileage-per-year and risk buckets; the train/test split is stratified to preserve the 12.35 % bad-buy rate. Models use balanced class weights, and the decision threshold is tuned on the F1 curve.

What drives a bad buy

The single most predictive feature — consistent across Logistic Regression (L1) and Random Forest — is a missing wheel type (WheelType = Unknown), followed by specific models/sub-models and vehicle age. A data-quality gap doubles as a risk signal.

Recommendation

Random Forest with balanced class weights is the strongest candidate. Because bad-buy precision is still modest (~0.31), deploy it as a triage filter that flags risky offers for human review — not as an automatic reject — and treat a missing WheelType as a first-order risk indicator at intake.
